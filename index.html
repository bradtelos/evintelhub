Tesla — Supercharger (DCFC) + Wall Connector for Business (L2)
Two distinct products — different use cases, same closed platform
Deep evaluate
Product 1 — Supercharger (DCFC, highway/destination)
NACS connector is the dominant US standard. Tesla Supercharger network now open to non-Tesla vehicles. Best-in-class UX and reliability. Commercial/developer partnership terms opaque — Tesla does not pay rent to site owners for Supercharger deployments and does not share charging data with the site host.
Product 2 — Wall Connector for Business (L2, long-dwell)
A distinct commercial program targeting apartments, hotels, workplaces, and retail — exactly the long-dwell use cases that complement DCFC. Key mechanics: property owner buys and installs the hardware (~$650/unit + ~$5,000 estimated turnkey installation per unit), Tesla provides the software platform, management portal, billing, and network listing at no ongoing cost. If pay-per-use is enabled, Tesla takes $0.03/kWh and remits the rest to the property owner quarterly. If charging is free to users, the software is entirely free. Sites are listed on Tesla's Find Us map and Trip Planner — putting your property in front of millions of active Tesla drivers planning routes. The Universal Wall Connector supports non-Tesla EVs via built-in J1772 adapter, and NACS adoption is now spreading to Rivian, Ford, and others. Program is actively scaling: 1,250+ new commercial connectors added since November 2025, with 158+ new sites globally. Tesla also released a Tall Pedestal (Feb 2026) for freestanding outdoor commercial installs.
Strategic assessment — the "all-Tesla platform" thesis
The concept of pairing Tesla Superchargers (DCFC) with Tesla Wall Connectors for Business (L2) on the same properties is compelling on paper — one brand, one app, one platform, one set of installer relationships. The Wall Connector program is genuinely attractive for multifamily, hotel, and retail properties in your portfolio where dwell time is 1+ hours. The economics are straightforward: you own the hardware and keep session revenue minus $0.03/kWh, with no ongoing software fee. However, the closed ecosystem is the central strategic risk. If you go all-Tesla, you are locked into Tesla's platform, pricing, and terms with no leverage — and the OCPP situation (detailed below) makes this a meaningful concern for your long-term operating flexibility.
OCPP status — the critical question
Tesla Wall Connectors currently do not officially support OCPP. Tesla's platform is proprietary — chargers communicate through Tesla's closed cloud and app, not an open OCPP backend. This means you cannot manage Tesla Wall Connectors through a third-party CSMS like Monta, ChargeLab, or Red E Enterprise. You are fully dependent on Tesla's portal and tools. As of early 2026, there are early signals of OCPP support coming: firmware updates have included references to OCPP APIs (noted by developer communities in late 2025), and at least one review source flagged "early signs of OCPP support" in 2025 firmware. Tesla has reportedly confirmed OCPP 1.6 support is in development for the Universal Wall Connector via firmware update, but no official public documentation or timeline has been released. Do not build your operational plan around this unless Tesla formally confirms it.
Management platform details (current)
Two portals: Powerhub (real-time charger status, uptime monitoring) and Tesla Business Portal (billing records, pricing configuration, session data download). Notable limitations per operators in the field: no per-user identification (you know a session happened, not who charged), no access denial by individual vehicle, no idle fee functionality. Management software is described as rudimentary but improving.

Software & protocols
OCPP explained, why it matters for your model, and key CSMS vendors to evaluate
OCPP — Open Charge Point Protocol
The universal communication standard between chargers and management systems
OCPP is the open-source protocol that defines how EV charging stations communicate with a central management system (CSMS). Think of it as the common language between hardware and software — similar to how cell phones communicate with carrier networks. Developed in 2009, maintained by the Open Charge Alliance (OCA), and now an IEC international standard (IEC 63584, ratified 2024).
Why this matters for you as an operator and developer
OCPP-compliant hardware means you are never locked into a single software vendor. You can swap your management platform without replacing your chargers — which protects your investment as the software market matures and consolidates. It also makes your sites NEVI-eligible (federal NEVI program requires OCPP compliance), which is critical for accessing grant funding. Always require OCPP compliance in any hardware or software vendor agreement.
Version history — what to know
OCPP 2.1 — Current (released January 2025)
Latest version. Adds Vehicle-to-Grid (V2G) support, bidirectional charging, distributed energy resource (DER) control, and improved smart charging. Backward compatible with 2.0.1. The version to design toward for future-proof infrastructure.
OCPP 2.0.1 — Current standard (released 2020, IEC-ratified 2024)
The modern baseline. Introduces advanced device management, smart charging profiles, Plug & Charge (ISO 15118), improved security with TLS encryption, and V2G groundwork. The version most new deployments are targeting today.
OCPP 1.6 — Legacy but widely deployed (released 2015)
Still the most widely installed version in the field. Functional for basic operations but lacks advanced security, smart charging, and V2G support. Acceptable for near-term deployments but not 2.0.1-compatible — a future migration would be needed. Avoid specifying this for new builds.
Key protocols to know alongside OCPP
OCPI — Open Charge Point Interface. Handles roaming and data exchange between different charging networks (horizontal). Enables drivers to use one app across multiple networks.
ISO 15118 — Vehicle-to-charger communication standard. Enables Plug & Charge (no app or RFID needed — just plug in) and V2G bidirectional power flow.
NACS — North American Charging Standard. Tesla's connector format, now the US industry standard for hardware. Separate from OCPP (connector vs. communication protocol).
CSMS vendors — Charging Station Management Software
The CSMS is the software layer that sits between your chargers and your business operations — it handles remote monitoring, session management, billing, access control, load management, and reporting. Connectivity
WiFi and cellular solutions for bringing EV chargers online — hardware, carriers, costs, and site decision framework
Key framing — data usage is minimal
EV chargers send telemetry, session data, and management pings — not streaming video. Realistic usage is 1–5 GB/month per site regardless of stall count. This keeps cellular data costs very low and makes connectivity a rounding error in your operating budget. Carry $1,500/site capex and $40/month recurring as your underwriting line items.
Site decision framework — in order of cost efficiency
Run through these three steps at every new site before spending anything on connectivity
1
Test existing hotel / property WiFi at stall locations — cost: $0
Tesla Gen 3 Wall Connectors need a WPA2-secured 2.4GHz network — standard enterprise hotel WiFi meets this spec. Ask: "Can you add our chargers' MAC addresses to a dedicated VLAN or network segment?" Many hotel IT teams will cooperate, especially branded properties (Hilton, Marriott) being pushed by their flag to expand EV. What kills it: security policies blocking non-guest devices, or parking lots out of AP range. Always test signal at proposed stall locations before budgeting anything.
2
Check T-Mobile and Verizon 5G coverage at the specific address — cost: ~$40/month
If strong signal is confirmed → deploy a Peplink BR1 Pro 5G with an IoT data plan. This is your default solution for the vast majority of sites in the Cincinnati MSA and target markets. Carrier-agnostic, deployment-ready, one-time capex.
3
Dedicated ISP line — only if cellular coverage is genuinely poor
Requires hotel/property operator cooperation for ISP provisioning access. Monthly cost ~$50–80/month for a business broadband line. Higher upfront coordination cost, longer lead time. Last resort only — most urban/suburban sites in your target markets will have strong 5G.
